Absolute Collagen (academic UX project)

web shop UX re-design + UI touch-up

In my second year of design school, I was in a three-person team tasked with redesigning Absolute Collagen’s mobile webshop. The goal was to redesign their mobile webshop. The audience was women age 25-60, mostly middle-class with interest with wellness and beauty.

I took on the product page—everything from first impressions, searching for info, adding to cart to UI consistency—and also took over the menu redesign after a teammate left (happends...).

Design Process

The design process was quite extensive so I aim to cover only key steps that mostly influenced the outcomes and my learning.

Key stages

1. Research

1.1 How did we know what's the problem?

User Interviews
On a student budget, I roped in classmates for user-interview and observed their first interactions with the site. I observed:

  • Overwhelming amount of information per page
  • No intuitive categorisation in menu & product page
  • Adding to card a subscription was a bit confusing (how many will I get? When? How much will it be?)

Google Analytics
I had limited access but few issues were critical:

  • Very slow loading
  • Low engagement with educational content

UX Heuristic
I leaned on Baymard Institute and Nielsen Norman heuristics.

  • No higlighted product benefits
  • Comfirmed all of the above

1.2 Card Sorting

To see how people naturally group content and what actually feels intuitive for navigation and information structure on the product page. I used Optimal Workshop for a card-sorting exercise with a small but telling group of 16 participants. I used those findings for creating next itterations.

1.3 Navigation structure

I saw how competition that performs well is making their menu. This also aligned with my card-sorting findings. I decided not to reinvent the wheel here and use this simple new structure that emarged from my findings. Now I just needed to flesh it out effectively.

Learning Opportunity

In my card sorting reaserch I tried to keep things short by replacing “Absolute Collagen” with “X.” Seemed smart at the time. It wasn’t. The statements became confusing, people dropped out, and the task lost clarity. Lesson learned: clarity always beats clever shorthand. I ended up repeating the research.

1.4 New Product Information Structure

The original product page had a lot of good content, but it was doing too much at once. Important information was buried, and users had to work harder than they should to understand the product and how to buy it. I reorganized the page around how users actually look for information - optimise it for scanning.

1.5 Applying heuristics for lesser issues elements

Not every problem needed fresh user research. For smaller UX elements, I relied on Baymard Institute heuristics using their Figma plugin to make quick, informed improvements. This covered things like adding lifestyle imagery to show the product in context, improving how additional product photos are displayed etc.

2. Prototyping (add-to-cart)

Navigation and the product page were fairly straightforward to prototype. Once I understood the information structure users expected, implementation came naturally.

Because of that, I focused the prototyping phase on the add-to-cart flow with the subscription option, which turned out to be the most complex (and interesting) part of the project.



2.1 Requirement: Purchase & Subscription Models

This part was by far the hardest. The business logic was complex, but the UI had to feel effortless and obvious to the user.

  • One-time purchase + two subscription options (every 2 weeks / every 4 weeks)
  • Each option had different prices and box quantities
  • The challenge was making these differences easy to understand at a glance
  • Few good real-world examples existed, so the solution had to be designed from scratch

2.2 Exploring and testing different approaches

2.3 I thought I struck gold, but it didn't work...

After a lot of testing, none of the early solutions really worked. I found an approach online that made sense, but when reviewed with the lead designer, it proved too heavy and space-hungry for mobile. We parked it and kept iterating.

2.4 Final design

The final design isn’t perfect, but it performs solidly. I arrived at this solution through countless iterations and close alignment with Baymard Institute recommendations. I’m still not fully convinced it’s one that works reasonably well within real constraints.

3. UI Design

3.1 Brand Consistency

UI had multiple visual directions across the site, so I streamlined it by choosing a single primary yellow, unifying buttons, icons, and typography, and designing flexible assets for infographics and social media. The goal was a clean, modern look with a luxurious feel, making the site consistent, engaging, and accessible for the audience.

4. Testing

By the time the functional prototype was ready, many students had already left and the deadline was tight—so there was no proper final testing. If time had allowed, I would have focused on:

  • Task-based usability testing to validate understanding of one-time vs subscription options

  • Misclick and rage-click analysis (via tools like Google Analytics / Hotjar) to spot confusion and friction

  • Performance checks (load time, perceived speed on mobile)

Learning Opportunity

Next time I would leave more time for final testing. Especially to test subscription logic. Even well-researched patterns still need real-world testing when multiple pricing models are present.
